kālo means time and also it means death. It is usually an attribute of Lord Shiva, manifest as Kalabhairava, the Lord of Time or Death. But He is also manifest in the aspect of Kalantaka, the ender of time. But also the ender of death ( the ender of ending - he who conquers death and transcends time ). However above it is Lord Krishna in the form of Vishvarupa, who is professing that “I am Time, the maker of the destruction of worlds, fully arisen, engaged here in annihilating the worlds.” For those who do not know, all the deities in Hinduism ( at least according to serious schools of 
Hindu philosophy ) are ultimately One and the same single ultimate God or Reality or Existence. 
Or at least, the major ones ( not all the million gods in everyone's backyard). 








People often times embark on a path only if certain conditions are met. There are 
those who must understand first (or at least to become convinced by theoretical solidity), 
there are those who must believe first (or become convinced by a more emotional way)
and there are those who require neither since they are just curious and naturally enthusiastic. 

For those who must understand, the entire Philosophy section is dedicated to just that. 

For those who must believe, they must find it elsewhere. 

But for those who made up their mind already, they surely want to experience (not 
just understand or believe). So this is where we begin. 


There are three paths (mārga) to liberation, known as Trimārga:

Karma-mārga - 'Path of Action'

This is doing what needs to be done. It is for those who live in the world, have work to do, a job, a family, responsibilities, they simply won't have the time. They must either believe or be curious so that they follow their practice else they won't reach anywhere. 

Bhakti-mārga - 'Path of Devotion'

This is the path of the more classical mysticism, where visualizations and imagery can be easily be evoked, gods are "consacrated" and forms are being made up to directly penetrate into the Unconscious and bypass the subconscious mind's defenses. It's emotional, people pray, cry and love. They become children and play their way into the deeper Truth. Emotion attaches to form so hence we use forms. 

It is here that two different concepts must be introduced. 

bhāvanā

In Sanskrit it means “bringing into being”. Oftentimes translated as "meditation", this word derives from the root "bhū" which means "to be" or "to become" and has been understood or misunderstood (mostly) as cultivating, producing, manifesting, imagining, even reflecting. 

It is in the first sense, that of cultivation, that the term is used to mean the sustained development of particular states of mind. Yet unless done properly it can just be a tool for self-delusion and fantasy imagination which is so popular today in the circles of new age pop spirituality. At best
it can work as placebo, but at its worst it can be debilitating and most alienating from true spiritual fulfillment.

kalpanā

 Katha Upanishad tells us that virtual objects exist only during kalpanā-kāla i.e. during the period of imagination, owing to avidyā .[9] And, according to Patanjali, kalpanā ('fancy') is more subjective than illusion and hallucination.




There are two major schools: bhāvana or kalpanā. This uses the mind intentionally: you visualize
gods, chakras, light, sounds, breath as energy, etc. It is essential in Tantra and in some forms of yoga
 this is quite central. But this is no play, you repeat it until the mind believes (the point is to 
 bypass the mind) and then the body shall react. With time, these become concrete experiential realities (warmth, vibrations, strong emotions, presence, etc. ) This works amazing for those with
 a strong vivid imagination and outbursting with emotion, the highly suggestible kind (which can also
 be easily hypnotized) - however - there are risks. The major risk is self-suggestion and fantasy unless
 proper guidance by a guru or spiritual master along with strogn discipline and discernment is applied. 








Jñāna-mārga - 'Path of Knowledge'

This is the path of attaining direct seeing (vidyā) without any attachments, without any 
forms and without mental devices. Imagery is forbidden in favor of direct observation. 
"Who am I" is the question and then taking away of identifications one by one instead of adding knowledge, it is all about removing false knowledge until only truth (nothingness) remains. 
This is where the Upanishads and Advaita Vedanta are the foundational guidances. It is also the 
method used by Zen Buddhism, Dzogchen and certain forms of Vipassana. 

This is the path that not everyone can walk on, it requires dedication, solitude, meditative states
and a certain kind of mind which slowly allows and opens up to the annihilation of all falsity.
